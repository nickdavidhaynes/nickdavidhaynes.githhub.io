Research

My work in one sentence

I build networks out of digital electronics to study the way that large, interdependent systems behave, and in particular, whether artificial systems can learn to perform tasks that are considered "intelligent."

A somewhat longer overview

My research is all about exploring the intersection between machine learning, complex networks, and the physics of information. But what does that mean?

Complex, highly interconnected systems are all around us - think about your brain, the internet, or the global economy. What all of these have in common is that when huge number of individual agents (like neurons, computers, or businesses/consumers) interact with each other in relatively simple ways, immensely complicated behavior emerges. The emerging interdisciplinary field of network science studies systems like these to better understand why they behave the way they do and to try to predict when they will behave well (or catastrophically!).

Arguably, one of the problems with studying complex systems is that, well, they're complex. This means that building systems to do experiments on is hard. For this reason, most of the effort in network science right now is focused on observing networks that already exist and developing theoretical and computational tools to try to explain their behavior. What's generally missing are experimental platforms that are easy to build and reconfigure, but complicated enough to produce interesting behavior that generalizes to real-world systems.

In the Quantum Electronics Lab at Duke, we use integrated circuits called field-programmable gate arrays (FGPAs) to build complex networks of digital electronics. A typical FPGA contains anywhere from thousands to millions of discrete digital logic elements that can be wired together arbitrarily to form complex networks with nearly any desired structure.
